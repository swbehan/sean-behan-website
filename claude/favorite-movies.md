# My Favorite Movies & Why I Love Them

---

## Oppenheimer
*Christopher Nolan · 2023 · Music by Ludwig Göransson*

Christopher Nolan has this extraordinary ability to take a single human being and make you feel the full historical weight of their existence. Oppenheimer is the peak of that. Every decision J. Robert Oppenheimer makes — every doubt, every compromise, every moment of brilliance — ripples outward into the world we live in today. Nolan never lets you forget that. The film doesn't just tell you the stakes, it makes you feel them in your chest.

I love history, so this movie was made for me. But what elevates it beyond any documentary or textbook is how deeply personal it stays. This is one man and his troubles, his contradictions, his triumphs — and Nolan frames all of it so that you understand you are watching the moment the modern world was born. Ludwig Göransson's score is relentless and unsettling in exactly the right way, building tension that never fully releases even when the bomb goes off.

---

## Interstellar
*Christopher Nolan · 2014 · Music by Hans Zimmer*

A completely different feeling from Oppenheimer, but the same genius at work. From the opening scenes Nolan makes you feel the weight of a dying world — the dust, the quiet resignation, the sense that humanity is running out of time. By the time Cooper climbs into that cockpit, the stakes feel so real and so enormous that you carry them with you for the entire runtime.

Hans Zimmer's score is one of the greatest ever composed for film. The organ swells, the ticking, the way the music seems to breathe in and out like something alive — you have chills from beginning to end. Nolan and Zimmer together create an experience where the sound and the image are inseparable.

My favorite scene is the docking sequence after Dr. Mann blows up his ship trying to dock without the airlock. Cooper manually wrestling a spinning, damaged vessel back into alignment — with that score underneath it — is one of the most viscerally tense and exhilarating sequences I have ever watched. No CGI spectacle, no explosion, just precision and desperation and Hans Zimmer. Nothing else comes close.

## Project Hail Mary
*In the same vein as Interstellar*

This one means something different to me than the others on this list. It isn't about romantic love or saving the world in a grand heroic sense — it's about a friendship between two beings who have absolutely no reason to trust each other and every reason to be afraid of each other, and who choose connection anyway.

The relationship between Ryland Grace and Rocky is one of the most touching things I've experienced in any story. They come from completely different worlds — different biology, different physics, different everything — and yet they find a way to understand each other, to sacrifice for each other, to genuinely care. That's the whole movie. It quietly argues that connection is the most important thing there is, and it makes that argument without ever being sentimental or forced about it.

The fishing scene is burned into my memory. The visuals, the action, the emotion all collide at once in a way that felt completely unlike anything I'd seen before. The music lands it perfectly. This movie is a reminder that science fiction at its best is really just a story about what it means to care about someone else.

## The Lego Movie ❤️ Guilty Pleasure
*Phil Lord & Christopher Miller · 2014*

I have loved this movie since the day it came out and I will never apologize for it. What makes it work — and what keeps it working even now that I'm older — is that the humor never feels manufactured. It comes out of the world naturally, and the world itself is so thoroughly committed to being made of Lego that it never breaks the spell for a single frame. The water is Lego. The fire is Lego. The explosions are Lego. It's so purely itself.

The comedy is genuinely smart without being smug about it, which is a rare thing. But underneath all the jokes and the visual inventiveness is a story that is quietly symbolic and genuinely heartwarming. The ending reframes everything in a way that lands even harder the more times you've seen it. It's a movie that respects both the kids watching it and the adults in the room, and it pulls that balance off better than almost anything else in the genre. Everything is awesome — and for this movie, it actually is.
