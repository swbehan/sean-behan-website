# My Favorite Games & Why I Love Them

---

## The Last of Us Part I
*Naughty Dog · 2013 / Remastered 2022*

This game redefined what storytelling in the medium could look like. The relationship between Joel and Ellie unfolds with such quiet authenticity — it never tells you how to feel, it just puts you there. The world is brutally realized and the performances carry weight that most films never reach. The tension between survival and humanity is woven into every mechanic, every scavenged bullet, every quiet moment between characters. It set the foundation for everything that came after and proved once and for all that video games could tell stories worth taking seriously.

---

## The Last of Us Part II ⭐ My All-Time Favorite
*Naughty Dog · 2020*

My favorite game ever made. What gets me most is Gustavo Santaolalla's score — it's so stripped back, sometimes just a single guitar string plucked in a quiet room, yet it carries the full emotional weight of everything happening on screen. The simplicity of the music paired with the sheer depth of the story is something I've never experienced anywhere else.

The story is genuinely brave. It forces you to walk miles in the shoes of someone you hate, and by the end you understand that revenge hollows out everyone it touches. The whole game is a meditation on how the cycle of violence never resolves anything — you just keep losing more. The fan base split right down the middle because the game refuses to give you what you came for. That's exactly why it's a masterpiece. It trusts the player to sit with discomfort and come out the other side changed.

## God of War (2018) & God of War: Ragnarök ⭐ Favorites
*Santa Monica Studio · 2018 / 2022*

Bear McCreary's score hits like a thunderclap and then pulls back into something gentle and aching whenever it's just Kratos and Atreus alone in the wilderness. That contrast is the whole game in miniature.

Here is a man who has spent his entire existence defined by rage and violence — a god of war in the most literal sense — and he has absolutely no idea how to just be a father. Watching him learn, stumble, say the wrong thing, and slowly open up across two massive games is one of the most genuinely moving character arcs I've ever experienced. The combat rewards aggression but demands patience, and every swing of the Leviathan Axe feels satisfying in a way that's almost impossible to put into words. It's a beautiful story of a father and son that sneaks up on you and completely breaks you by the end.

## Ghost of Tsushima
*Sucker Punch Productions · 2020*

No game I've played looks quite like this. The wind bending through tall grass, the way light cuts through bamboo, the ink-wash skies — it feels like playing inside a painting. The landscape alone makes it one of my favorites.

The stance-switching combat system is where I keep coming back. Reading a group of enemies, identifying the mix of shields and spears and brutes, and switching stances on the fly makes every encounter feel like a puzzle with a deeply satisfying solution. It's one of the most refreshing combat mechanics I've experienced in any action game. The story is more conventional than the other games I love, but it doesn't need to be groundbreaking when everything around it is this stunning.

---

## Fortnite
*Epic Games · playing since I was 12*

This one is personal in a way the others aren't. Since I was 12 years old, this game has been the place where my brothers and I come together regardless of where we are in the world. That alone would be enough to earn its place on this list.

But the reason it keeps working after all these years is because Epic has never let it go stale. The loot pool is always shifting, the collabs keep arriving, the map keeps evolving. They found a way to make a battle royale feel like it has a living, breathing identity that grows with you. It's not just a game — it's been a constant thread through my entire teenage and young adult life, a way to stay connected to the people I love most.

---

## Sea of Thieves
*Rare · 2018*

What Sea of Thieves does that almost nothing else does is make you feel genuinely vulnerable. Spending eight hours hauling treasure across the open ocean and then hearing cannon fire on the horizon creates a real, physical tension that most games can only simulate. You have something to lose, and that changes everything.

When ships clash — cannons blazing, planks splintering, crew scrambling below deck to bail water — it feels cinematic in the truest sense, like you fell into a pirate film. The world is sun-drenched and gorgeous, the stories tucked into the voyages are genuinely touching, and the simplicity of the core loop — sail, explore, dig, fight, sell — is endlessly satisfying. The ever-present threat of real players in real lobbies ready to take everything you worked for gives the whole experience a tension that no scripted encounter ever could.
